CONCLUSION

DATA BREACHES DURING COVID-19

The COVID-19 pandemic has caused an increase in online activity as we see more people working from home, opting for virtual doctors appointments, online schooling, etc. With this increased online activity there has also been reports of an increase in cyber attacks. One specific example comes from the World Health Organization (WHO), which reported a fivefold increase in cyberattacks in late April 2020. Hundreds of WHO staff memebers' email addresses and passwords were recently linked. WHO also reports an increase in spam emailing directed at its staff. In October, there was also an increase of cyber attacks in the research and pharmaceutical industry as development of the COVID-19 vaccination took place.

HANDLING DATA BREACHES IN 2020

Use strong and secure passwords

Billions of consumers' login information is compromised in data breaches every year. This is why it is important to use different passwords for each account, and change your passwords immediately if a company you've recently interacted with gets hacked.

Has my password been stolen?

Monitor your bank and other financial accounts

Check your accounts on a regular basis for unfamiliar activity. And if the companies offer activity alerts via text or email, it may make sense for you to sign up for them.

Guard against identity theft

Globally, 65% of data breaches result in identity theft, making it the most common outcome. Checking your credit report regularly and considering a credit monitoring/identity theft protection program may be a good idea.
